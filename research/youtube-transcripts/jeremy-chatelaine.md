# Jeremy Chatelaine – The Perfect Cold Email Structure That Gets Replies

## Executive Summary

Jeremy Chatelaine, founder of QuickMail, shares a data-backed framework for writing cold emails that consistently generate replies in today's increasingly competitive outbound environment. Drawing from millions of emails sent through QuickMail, he explains that successful cold emails aren't about clever copywriting—they're about relevance, targeting, value, and reducing friction throughout the buying journey.

## Key Takeaways

- Every cold email must pass three filters:

  - Spam filters (deliverability)

  - Inbox scan (gets opened)

  - Content evaluation (earns a reply)

- Keep greetings simple and avoid overly formal openings like "Dear."

- The intro line is the most important part of the email—it should immediately answer, "Why are you contacting me?"

- AI-generated personalization is becoming easy for buyers to recognize and often reduces authenticity.

- Good targeting is more valuable than clever copywriting.

- Value propositions should focus on the recipient's outcome, not your product features.

- Keep emails concise and discuss one idea per email.

- Strong social proof increases credibility without sounding boastful.

- CTAs should ask for small commitments that are easy to complete.

- Replies improve future deliverability more than simply optimizing for spam filters.

## Actionable Insights

### 1. Build Every Email Around the Three Filters

Before sending, ask:

- Will it reach the inbox?

- Will someone open it?

- Will they see enough value to reply?

Optimizing all three stages dramatically improves results.

---

### 2. Write Better Intro Lines

Your opening should immediately explain why you're reaching out.

Examples:

- "I noticed you're hiring SDRs."

- "We're working with B2B SaaS founders in Austin."

- "Richard Parker suggested I reach out."

Avoid generic openers like:

- Hope you're doing well.

- Happy Monday.

- Just checking in.

---

### 3. Improve Targeting Before Improving Copy

If you're struggling to write relevant first lines, your prospect list is probably too broad.

Instead of improving personalization:

- Narrow your ICP

- Segment by industry

- Segment by role

- Segment by common problems

Better targeting naturally produces better messaging.

---

### 4. Focus on Recipient Value

Ask:

> "What's in it for them?"

Instead of describing your company:

- Explain outcomes.

- Solve a specific problem.

- Demonstrate immediate relevance.

The recipient—not the sender—defines what is valuable.

---

### 5. Keep Emails Short

Each email should communicate:

- One idea

- One offer

- One next step

If you have multiple points, save them for follow-up emails.

---

### 6. Use Low-Friction CTAs

Instead of asking:

> "Can we schedule a 30-minute call?"

Try:

- "Reply 'Yes' and I'll send the report."

- "Interested in seeing the benchmark?"

- "Would it help if I shared the framework?"

Small commitments generate more responses.

---

### 7. Strengthen Your Signature

Use your signature to reinforce credibility with:

- LinkedIn profile

- Book or publication

- Awards

- Industry recognition

- TV appearances

- Product badges

Only include links that increase trust.

---

### 8. Add a Human PS

Use the postscript to build rapport.

Examples:

- Congratulations on a recent funding round.

- Good luck with your upcoming conference.

- Nice work on your recent product launch.

The PS reminds recipients they're communicating with a real person.

---

### 9. Leverage Attachments and Thumbnails

When appropriate:

- Attach PDFs

- Include images

- Share YouTube videos

Email clients often generate preview thumbnails that increase curiosity and engagement.

---

### 10. Follow Core Cold Email Principles

Jeremy's recommended rules:

- Write like you're speaking to a real person.

- Keep emails short.

- Focus on the recipient—not yourself.

- Use plain formatting.

- Present one clear idea per email.

- Avoid URL shorteners.

- Avoid unnecessary punctuation and formatting.

- Treat cold email as the beginning of a conversation—not a hard sales pitch.
