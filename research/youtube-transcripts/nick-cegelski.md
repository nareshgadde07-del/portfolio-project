# nick cegelski — The "Mr. Miyagi" Method for Cold Calls

## Executive Summary

This video explains why most objections during cold calls are not genuine rejections of your product—they're emotional reactions to being interrupted. Using the "Mr. Miyagi Method," the speaker demonstrates how agreeing with objections, lowering sales pressure, and encouraging prospects to elaborate can turn dismissive responses into productive sales conversations.

# Main Ideas & Themes

- Why cold call objections are usually reactions to interruption
- Emotional vs. logical objection handling
- The "Mr. Miyagi Method" for disarming resistance
- Pattern interruption through agreement
- Using curiosity instead of persuasion
- Asking multiple-choice follow-up questions
- Turning dismissive objections into conversations
- Reintroducing value after lowering resistance
- Building trust without increasing sales pressure

---



# Key Takeaways

- Most prospects are rejecting the interruption—not your product.
- Common objections like:
  - "I'm not interested."
  - "Call me in six months."
  - "Send me an email."
  are usually knee-jerk emotional responses.
- Trying to overcome emotional objections with logic often makes the conversation worse.
- Don't immediately defend your product or pitch harder.
- Use the **Mr. Miyagi Method**:
  1. Agree with the objection.
  2. Remove sales pressure.
  3. Encourage the prospect to explain further.
- Agreeing with objections creates a pattern interrupt because it is unexpected.
- Prospects become more willing to talk once they feel they are no longer being sold to.
- Give prospects an incentive to elaborate by explaining that you want to avoid bothering them again unnecessarily.
- Use multiple-choice follow-up questions to make responding easier.
- The objective isn't to overcome the objection immediately—it's to reopen the conversation.
- Curiosity is more effective than confrontation.
- Once the prospect shares more context, you can tailor your response instead of guessing.
- "Send me an email" should become an opportunity to discover what information is actually relevant.
- Introduce a **problem proposition** after lowering resistance to reconnect the conversation with a business problem.
- Better objection handling leads to:
  - More conversations
  - Better discovery
  - Higher-quality meetings
  - Less pressure-filled selling

# Actionable Insights



### 1. Stop Treating Every Objection as a Final "No"

Recognize that most initial objections are simply reactions to an unexpected interruption.

Respond calmly instead of becoming defensive.

---



### 2. Agree Before Explaining

Instead of arguing, validate the prospect's response.

Examples:

- "That's totally fair."
- "You're probably right."
- "This one's on me."

Agreement lowers emotional resistance.

---



### 3. Remove Sales Pressure

Make it clear your goal is understanding—not forcing a sale.

For example:

> "Just so nobody from my team keeps bothering you..."

This changes the dynamic from persuasion to curiosity.

---



### 4. Ask Multiple-Choice Follow-Up Questions

Instead of asking broad questions:

> "Why?"

Offer simple choices:

- Is it budget?
- Is it timing?
- Is it because you already have a solution?
- Or do you simply dislike cold calls?

People answer multiple-choice questions more easily than open-ended ones.

---



### 5. Use Objections to Gather Information

Every objection contains useful context.

Rather than overcoming it immediately, learn:

- Buying timeline
- Existing solutions
- Budget process
- Business priorities

This information improves future conversations.

---



### 6. Reintroduce Your Value Naturally

Once the prospect opens up, reconnect your solution to a specific business problem they likely face.

Lead with the problem—not your product.

---



### 7. Replace Pressure with Curiosity

Avoid responses like:

- "Let me explain..."
- "Actually..."
- "You're wrong..."

Instead ask thoughtful follow-up questions that invite discussion.

---



### 8. Make "Send Me an Email" Productive

Instead of ending the conversation:

Ask:

> "What would be most useful for me to include?"

This gives you insight into their interests while increasing the likelihood they'll read your email.

---



### 9. Focus on Conversations, Not Immediate Meetings

The goal of objection handling is to:

- Keep the conversation alive
- Learn more about the buyer
- Earn the opportunity for a meeting later

---



### 10. Build a Repeatable Objection Framework

Practice responses for common objections such as:

- "I'm not interested."
- "No budget."
- "Call me later."
- "Send me an email."

Using a consistent framework improves confidence and conversation quality over time.
